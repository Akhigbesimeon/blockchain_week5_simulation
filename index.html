Scenario
An NGO is delivering food and medical aid to displaced persons in a conflict zone. Funds are disappearing, beneficiary identities can't be verified, and there's no agreed record of who received what. Run through three rounds to see how blockchain changes this.
How to run it
1
Go to Roles and assign each student or group their role before anything else.
2
Round 1 — Centralized. Submit some transactions. Let the Bad Actor tamper. Watch the Auditor struggle.
3
Round 2 — Blockchain. Now every transaction needs node consensus. Try the same tamper — the chain hash mismatch is detected automatically.
4
Round 3 — Smart Contract. Automated rules block unverified IDs, suspicious purposes, and oversized transfers before reaching the nodes at all.
5
End on Debrief — six questions to close the session.

Round 1 — Centralized

Single coordinator. No checks. Bad Actor can alter records silently. Auditor is almost always too late.

Round 2 — Blockchain

Every transaction broadcasts to all nodes. 3 of 4 must approve. Tampering breaks the chain hash.

Round 3 — Smart Contract

Automated rules enforce verified IDs, legitimate purposes, and a $1,000 transaction cap before a transaction even reaches the nodes.

Round 1 — Centralized
R1 Q1
When the Bad Actor tampered with the ledger, who noticed — and when? What would have happened if there was no Auditor watching at all?
R1 Q2
In real humanitarian aid delivery, which organizations play the role of the single coordinator? What are the risks of giving any one actor that level of unchecked control over the ledger?
Round 2 — Blockchain
R2 Q3
Why couldn't the Bad Actor tamper successfully in Round 2? What specifically broke — and how did the system detect it without any human auditor being involved?
R2 Q4
We used 4 nodes and required 3 to agree. What happens if one node is dishonest? What if two nodes collude? At what point does the consensus model actually fail?
Round 3 — Smart Contract
R3 Q5
The smart contract auto-rejected transactions with unverified IDs and amounts over the $1,000 cap. But who wrote those rules? Who can update them — and what happens if the rules themselves are biased against certain populations?
R3 Q6
Think about BanQu's refugee identity model. How would you adapt this three-round simulation for East Africa? Which round best represents what BanQu actually does — and what is still missing from our model?
